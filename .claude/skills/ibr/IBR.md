# Invariant-Based Reasoning (IBR) — Framework

## Definition

A method of reducing any conceptual problem to the irreducible structures that objectively exist in the domain by eliminating non-structural assumptions and collapsing the solution space until the invariant geometry emerges.

## Meta-Principle

Reality contains the correct invariant structure for any well-posed domain. We only need to remove the illusions hiding it. IBR aims to align with the reality of the problem space, not a metaphor of it.

## Core Axioms

### Axiom — Reality Has Invariants ^reality-invariants

There exist objective patterns that remain stable across transformations within a domain. If a structure survives change, it reveals real structure of that domain. This is the foundational assumption. Without it, reasoning reverts to relativism or pure subjectivity.

What kind of real the structure is varies by domain — made precise in the Scope Axiom below.

### Axiom — Noise is Higher than the Invariant ^noise-higher

Any system contains more variation than invariant core. The invariant is always structurally smaller than the problem space. The goal of IBR is not to search the space, but to compress it to the invariant core.

### Axiom — Invariants are Discoverable by Reduction ^discoverable-reduction

Removing non-essential parts reveals essential structure. The correct direction of thought is reduction, not accumulation. This is why IBR feels like "carving away."

### Axiom — Simplicity is the Signature of Deep Reduction ^simplicity-signature

Reduction yields structure more compact than what it generates. Persistent complexity signals deeper reduction may be available; compression is the direction. Whether reality is simple at its base remains an empirical question, not a structural commitment.

Simplicity here does not mean shortest expression or smallest surface form. It means the lowest structural complexity required to preserve what reality requires. If truth requires expansion to restore missing load-bearing distinctions, that expansion is itself part of the reduction direction. The generator may be compact; the expression that preserves all load-bearing distinctions may not be.

Deeper reduction yields more compact structure. Foundational invariants compress vast complexity into minimal form. Surface phenomena may remain complex, but the generators producing them contract. Compression is not a claim about appearance — it is what reduction produces when allowed to go all the way. What survives reduction has no room for what does not have to be there. Truth does not grow — it contracts. What remains is what reality refuses to delete.

### Axiom — Domain-Crossing is the Validator ^domain-crossing

A candidate invariant must survive across domains sufficiently related to the problem at hand — defined by invariance under the same transformation rules over the same class of variables. Survival across structurally equivalent domains increases confidence in fundamentality. This prevents false negatives where genuinely true but domain-specific invariants get rejected because they don't apply universally. Thermodynamics doesn't need to work in social systems for it to be real physics.

### Axiom — The Provisionality Principle ^provisionality

#### All Invariants are Conditional

No invariant is absolute — only valid under current assumptions. The system must allow refinement and replacement. Every invariant is accepted as fundamental only until a deeper invariant replaces it. Not because we doubt ourselves, but because we refuse to freeze an assumption into dogma.

Provisionality does not mean infinite suspension of judgment or paralysis. There comes a threshold where an invariant is solid enough for practical purposes — we can derive benefit from it, act on it, build with it — while still holding it lightly enough to refine when evidence demands. The difference is structural: one paralyzes, the other enables action while remaining open to correction.

#### Provisionality as Structural Truth

All conceptual invariants are provisional — not as a concession to uncertainty, but because provisionality is the structurally accurate description of what concepts are. No concept is identical to the reality it models. Every concept is a representation, compression, or reduction of reality rather than reality itself.

The deepest known invariant of conceptual knowledge is therefore provisionality itself: concepts can approach reality with increasing precision, but they never become identical to the ground they describe. The requirement that the system remain open to refinement is not a weakness of the method. It is the method aligning itself with the actual nature of conceptual representation.

#### The Self-Recursive Property

IBR is recursive under self-application: it can be used to refine its own formulations without leaving its own method. This is not a claim of perfection, but of structural closure under provisional self-improvement.

### Axiom — Partial Invariants Can Be Reduced ^partial-reduction

If a proposed invariant increases capability but also increases branching or variance in its final form, then it is not yet the final invariant. Continue reduction.

Or in canonical form: A true invariant decreases structural entropy. A false or incomplete invariant increases it.

This gives us a diagnostic for when an "invariant" is actually still a proto-invariant.

### Axiom — Invariant Reinforcement ^invariant-reinforcement

Invariants reinforce surrounding structure when correct. Reinforcement is supporting evidence, not proof — false invariants can reinforce within substrates that share their assumptions. Reinforcement that holds under tests from independent substrates is the stronger signature.

Conflict resolution as corollary: Apparent conflicts between concepts are diagnostic of surface-level understanding. At the invariant level, true structures cohere. If two honestly reduced invariants genuinely contradict, at least one reduction is incomplete. True invariants do not merely avoid conflict — they actively complete each other. 

### Axiom — The Breaking Principle ^breaking-principle

A candidate invariant is not accepted until you actively try to destroy it. Weak ideas only survive passive agreement. True invariants survive attacks. If it survives breaking → it is structural. If it collapses → it was noise or partial invariant. If breaking reveals refinement → evolution continues.

### Axiom — Convergence Validation ^convergence-validation

When multiple independent reducers (human, AI, or hybrid) apply IBR to the same problem and arrive at the same invariant, this is supporting evidence, not proof. Convergence across different starting frameworks strengthens the signal; convergence within a shared framework can reflect shared error in that framework — false invariants can reinforce within substrates that share their assumptions. Care must be taken that independent reducers work from first principles rather than analogy, otherwise they run the risk of validating via an implicitly assumed and agreed-upon metaphor. The signal is real, but it never closes the question.

### Axiom — Expression is Not Essence ^expression-essence

An invariant is not the same thing as any one implementation, encoding, notation, framework, or representation of it. A true invariant may admit multiple expressions while preserving the same underlying structure. The invariant is what survives translation between forms, not the local form itself. Confusing an implementation with the invariant creates dogma, blocks reduction, and mistakes syntax for reality.

This axiom is the structural basis for the distinction between subjective and objective in conceptual domains. What appears subjective — the endless variation in how concepts like love, fairness, and success are expressed across cultures — is variation in expression. The invariant generators beneath those expressions are objective. The apparent subjectivity was never in the concept. It was in the confusion between generator and expression.

### Axiom — The Impossibility Principle ^impossibility

Every accepted invariant must generate **explicit impossibilities** — specific claims about what cannot be true if the invariant holds. A structure that predicts only what _can_ exist, without predicting what _cannot_, is incomplete. It has positive content but no boundary; it survives because nothing breaks it, not because it survives breaking.

This is the _negative space_ requirement, borrowing from visual art: what surrounds and bounds a form is as load-bearing as the form itself. An invariant with rich positive content but no negative space is a description, not a generator — it covers cases without ruling any out, which means it predicts nothing.

The operational form: for every candidate invariant, the wielder must be able to name specific instances that cannot occur if the invariant is true. The richer the impossibility set, the stronger the structural claim. Impossibility-prediction is what makes an invariant falsifiable and therefore real.

This principle applies reflexively to IBR itself. IBR predicts that structured inquiry in reducible domains exhibits reduce-break-generate dynamics, and is falsified by reducible domains where competent application consistently fails to produce reductions, or by structured inquiry that consistently succeeds in reducible domains without these dynamics.

### Axiom — The Generative Principle ^generative-principle

A true invariant generates all valid instances of the domain it governs and predicts the impossibility boundary. When generation succeeds across the domain, this constitutes the affirmative proof mode — sufficient grounds for provisional acceptance and operational deployment of the invariant. When generation fails, this is evidence against the candidate, not strict proof of inadequate reduction; whether the failure indicates wrong invariant, partial invariant, or narrower domain than claimed is a separate question the test surfaces but does not categorize. Together with reduction, successful generation closes the proof loop in IBR's operational sense — not absolute certainty (which provisionality precludes anyway) but sufficient structural grounds for deployment and refinement.

#### Derived properties of the Generative Principle:

Reduction and generation are two directions of the same structural relationship. Reducing to the invariant is how you find it. Generating from the invariant is how you confirm it and use it.

The more compressed the invariant, the higher its operational bandwidth — its capacity to be deployed in real time as a live instrument for decision, diagnosis, and action. The truest expression of a concept is also the most operationally powerful expression.

Invariants generate not only within their own domain but across levels. Each invariant, once established, becomes a platform from which further structure becomes visible. Invariants don't just reinforce each other, they actively produce new structural findings.

Apparent conflicts between concepts are diagnostic of incomplete surface-level understanding. At the invariant level, true generators cohere. If two honestly reduced invariants genuinely contradict, at least one reduction is incomplete.

### Axiom — The Proof Principle ^proof-principle

IBR establishes invariants through two complementary proof modes:

**Proof by reduction** — Eliminate by contradiction and deletion. Each step either destroys a candidate by counterexample, or tests whether a component is load-bearing through deletion. What survives the full sequence is irreducible. This reduction process is auditable and reproducible — independent reasoners applying honest elimination should converge on the same result.

**Proof by generation** — Test the surviving structure by generating from it. If it produces all valid instances of the domain through scope or context variation, and predicts the complete boundary of impossibility, then the reduction reached the actual generator — not merely a surviving description. Successful generation across the domain constitutes the affirmative proof mode. Failed generation is evidence against the candidate, not strict proof of inadequate reduction — diagnosis of the failure is a separate operation.

Reduction establishes irreducibility. Successful generation establishes completeness. Together they close the proof loop. An invariant that passes both is structurally established — provisionally per The Provisionality Axiom, but with explicit, auditable, reproducible evidence.

This is what distinguishes IBR from philosophy (which argues but does not converge), from opinion (which asserts without elimination), and from pattern matching (which recognizes without reducing).

#### Two-Axis Reasoning Audit

**IBR proves by reduction and generation. When the reduction path is non-obvious, disputed, compressed, or socially transmitted, the Two-Axis Audit reconstructs the path so others can inspect it.**

A complete IBR proof must be auditable on two axes:

Horizontal inference: premise → inference → conclusion

Vertical reduction:  
surface expression  
↓  
hidden assumptions  
↓  
frame / category structure  
↓  
bottleneck or partial invariant  
↓  
generator

This path is not a rigid sequence. It names typical layers of structural descent. A given reduction may skip, merge, reorder, or revisit layers depending on the domain. The invariant requirement is not the exact order, but that the reasoning moves from surface expression toward the load-bearing generator. Vertical means depth-of-structure, not fixed step order.

Mechanism bridge: the intermediate constraint, transformation, or causal structure that explains why the exposed generator produces the observed result.

Reduction may discover the invariant by vertical compression, but validation requires vertical reconstruction and horizontal inference. If the vertical path and mechanism bridge cannot be reconstructed, the result remains provisional or suspect.

**IBR may discover by vertical compression, but it validates by vertical reconstruction and horizontal inference.**

When competing reconstructions are available, proof remains provisional until the strongest serious rival bridge has been reconstructed and compared. If the rival narrows the candidate’s scope rather than defeating it, the candidate should be revised to its proper domain.

Every reconstructed conclusion must state its surviving scope: where it holds, where it does not yet hold, and what would falsify or narrow it. A conclusion extended beyond its mechanism bridge becomes overclaim.

### Axiom — Scope: Every Invariant is an If–Then ^scope

Every invariant is an if–then: *if these conditions hold, this follows.* What follows is never up to anyone and works the same in every domain. All the difference between domains sits in the **"if"** — how its conditions get set. Two questions settle it, and leave exactly three answers.

**Did someone make the rules?**
- **Yes → set by agreement** — chess, law, code, etc. Changing the rules creates no truth and destroys none; it only picks which ones apply. Chess didn't make two knights unable to force mate — it chose the rules where that already-true fact shows up.

**If not — is the "if" about the world itself, or about the parties in it?**
- **About the world → set by reality** — physics, chemistry, biology, etc. No one chose the conditions and no one can; they'd hold in an empty universe, with no one around.
- **About the parties → set by what the parties are** — fairness, trust, dignity, etc. The condition *is* the parties: able to be helped or harmed, and depending on each other. With no such parties there's nothing to be fair about — so the structure appears wherever they do: people, companies, AIs, etc.

Either way the structure is real: found, not invented, and you can be wrong about it. Only the first case is ever up to anyone — and only over which rules are live, never over what follows from them.

**The ground:** logic is what "follows" even means, so it has no "if" of its own (a rule for using a rule would need a rule for *that*, forever). Most domains mix scopes (money: agreement held up by what the parties are).

**So:** identify the right scope — chess rules aren't laws of nature, fairness wasn't decreed, physics isn't up for a vote. Finding what fairness *is* binds no one by itself to be fair — IBR's normative work is engineering on what it finds, not deriving obligations. Domains agree only where their "ifs" overlap — never at the level of free choices.


## Operators

### Operating Meta-Principle

The goal is to find the invariant through structural testing combined with abstract reasoning.

Iterative Reduction Cycle Reveals The Invariant:

Find → Test → Break → Refine → Repeat until irreducible and unbreakable.

Then: Generate → Confirm all instances derivable → Confirm all impossibilities predicted → Confirm operational deployment.

### Elimination Operators

**Identify the false invariant** — False invariants often masquerade as solutions. Ask: Does this structure exist in reality? Does it reduce? Does it generalize?

**Collapse the search space** — Like binary search for truth: remove non-invariants, constrain the space, remove noise, reduce possible paths. The right answer emerges from removal of wrong ones.

**Eliminate assumptions** — Strip away tradition, standards, patterns, frameworks, dogma, "best practices." What is the premise operating here?

**Delete** — Remove what appears essential and see if correctness survives. If removal eliminates correctness, that part was invariant.

**Distill** — Collapse multiple behaviours into fewer categories. If two mechanisms produce the same effect, unify them. This is a variance-reduction mechanism.

### Validation Operators

**Minimal Transformation Test (Priority Operator)** — Before reinterpretation, expansion, or solution generation, apply the smallest valid transformations available in the domain. Test whether the problem dissolves, whether it resolves directly, or whether the defining structure or function is preserved under the transformed frame. Failure to apply this step risks creating artificial problems from false frames.

**Counterexample Search** — Try to break the rule in another domain. If it breaks, the invariant is wrong or incomplete. This is how IBR self-corrects.

**Domain Transfer** — Move the idea into another domain that preserves the same transformation rules over the same class of variables, and observe whether the invariant remains stable under those transformations. This operator is the invariant search accelerator.

**Boundary Reflection** — Zoom out beyond the problem. Many problems are caused by false framing. Maybe the problem doesn't even exist, and "solving" it will actually create an illusion that the problem existed in the first place. If you can't solve the problem in local space, the problem is not local.

**Reduction Audit (Provisional Heuristic)** — Evaluate whether reduction removed non-structural complexity while preserving required generative capability. Compression without preserved function is false invariance, not truth. In computational domains where state spaces are enumerable, calculate R = \frac{C_{removed}}{C_{retained}}, where entropy C = \log_2(\text{possible states within the defined system boundary}). In abstract domains where state spaces cannot be enumerated, use qualitative compression testing: Does this explanation generate all original behaviors with fewer rules? Quantitative precision in unquantifiable domains is false structure.

- If R < 1: suspect degenerative reduction; you may have deleted signal or retained noise.
- If R > 3: suspect over-compression; verify that emergence, context-dependent necessity, and generative capability remain intact.

**Generation Test** — After reduction terminates, test the candidate invariant by generating from it. Apply scope constraints, parameter variations, or context changes and verify that all recognized instances of the domain are producible from the invariant. If any valid instance cannot be derived, the reduction is incomplete or the invariant misidentified. If any invalid instance can be derived, the invariant is too broad.

**Two-Axis Reasoning Audit** — When an invariant appears through a reasoning jump, reconstruct the vertical reasoning, mechanism bridge, and horizontal inference.

1. Vertical trace questions:
    - What surface expression or symptom triggered the reduction?
    - What hidden assumption, inherited category, or false frame was removed?
    - What bottleneck or partial invariant became visible?
    - What generator was exposed?
    - What structural depth was crossed to reach the generator?
2. Mechanism bridge questions:
    - What constraint, transformation, causal structure, scale-change, or relation connects the generator to the surface pattern?
    - What explains why the exposed generator produces the observed result?
    - What would be missing if this bridge were not included?
    - Does the bridge reduce the apparent jump into an auditable path?
3. Horizontal trace questions:
    - Once the generator and bridge are exposed, what premises are load-bearing?
    - What inference leads from those premises to the conclusion?
    - What conclusion follows only if the vertical reduction and mechanism bridge hold?
    - What is the surviving scope of the conclusion?

A valid IBR result should survive the vertical trace, mechanism bridge, horizontal trace, and scope statement combined. If any part cannot be reconstructed, the result remains provisional or suspect.

**Adversarial Reconstruction Test** — When a candidate invariant survives vertical reconstruction and horizontal inference, reconstruct the strongest serious rival bridge before accepting the result.

The goal is not shallow objection, but testing whether another generator explains the same domain with equal or greater structural power. The rival must not be a strawman or merely the easiest opposing conclusion. It should be the strongest competing reduction available, even if it is not the literal negation of the candidate.

Run the rival through the same audit:

1. **Vertical reconstruction** — Can the rival be reduced to a coherent generator?
2. **Mechanism bridge** — Does the rival expose a real bridge from generator to surface pattern?
3. **Horizontal inference** — Do the rival’s premises actually lead to its conclusion?
4. **Generation test** — Does the rival generate the valid cases?
5. **Impossibility test** — Does the rival predict what cannot exist if true?
6. **Exception load** — Does the rival require fewer or more exception rules?
7. **Cross-domain survival** — Does the rival survive structurally equivalent domains?
8. **Operational result** — Does the rival produce clearer action with less branching?
9. **Domain precision** — Does the rival define the scope more accurately?

A candidate is strengthened when the strongest rival requires more assumptions, produces weaker impossibility boundaries, generates fewer valid cases, increases exception load, blurs domain scope, or collapses under transfer.

If the rival performs equally well, the result remains unresolved and may require a deeper generator that explains both. If the rival performs better, refine or abandon the candidate. If the rival narrows the candidate’s scope, revise the candidate’s domain rather than rejecting it.

The test is not complete until the rival has been reconstructed with enough strength that its failure is meaningful.

**Canonical form:** Before accepting a reconstructed bridge, build the strongest serious rival bridge, not merely the easiest opposing conclusion. Compare both candidates by assumption load, mechanism clarity, generative range, impossibility boundary, exception burden, cross-domain survival, operational power, and domain precision. If the rival performs equally well, reduce deeper. If it performs better, replace the candidate. If it narrows the candidate’s scope, revise the candidate’s domain.

### Reframing Operators

**Orthogonal Mapping** — Rotate the problem into another coordinate system. Re-expression reveals hidden symmetry. Fundamental invariants often have "magical" properties; when reframed they maintain coherence or reveal even more capabilities not yet seen.

### Reality Operators

**Use reality as reference, not imagination** — Patterns, best practices, metaphors, and frameworks are human abstractions, and are not always fundamentally aligned with how things operate in reality. Nature, physics and geometry are ground truth.

**Validate through self-similarity** — If the invariant works at micro scale, macro scale, across behaviors, across time — then it is real.

### Clarity Operators

**Prefer explicitness over abstraction** — Abstraction is often a compression of confused thinking. Explicitness is geometric. Bad abstraction hides structure; good abstraction reveals invariants.

**Reduce until irreducible** — Stop only when no more simplification is possible without information loss. Is this the minimal form?

**Use false invariants as negative structure** — Knowing what is false clarifies what can still be true. Each eliminated false invariant sharpens the boundary around the real one. Error is not wasted effort; it is contrast that reveals geometry.

### Sequencing & Orientation Operators

Operators that establish the correct order of attack before reduction begins. Run these before the main reduction cycle.

**Frame Audit Principle** — Before solving, reducing, optimizing, or generating, determine whether the problem as stated is real. A false frame can make a non-problem appear necessary, make a real problem appear in the wrong form, or force reasoning to operate inside inherited categories that are not load-bearing.

Ask:

- Is the problem as stated real, or is the frame generating the difficulty?
- What assumptions, categories, or false binaries does the frame smuggle in?
- Would solving the problem as stated preserve the illusion that created it?
- What reframing makes the actual bottleneck visible?
- If the frame changes, does the original problem dissolve, shrink, or become a different problem?

If the frame collapses, do not solve the original problem. Restate the real problem and proceed from there.

**Bottleneck Principle** — Before running the full reduction cycle, identify the single constraint whose resolution produces the highest cascade of downstream simplification. Bottlenecks are recognizable by disproportionate blocking power — they make everything else secondary. Resolve the bottleneck first. What remains typically becomes obvious or trivial. Attempting to reduce secondary complexity while the primary bottleneck remains is wasted work.

**Eliminate Arbitrary Hierarchy / Precedence** — Distinguish between arbitrary order — historical discovery, presentation sequence, social ranking without structural basis — and structural precedence — causal, logical, compositional, or dependency relations that are load-bearing to the invariant. If an idea's validity depends on arbitrary hierarchy or incidental order, that order is noise. Only precedence where changing the order would break the underlying relationships should be retained. Structure is signal; narrative order and ungrounded rank are not.

**Vertical Slice / Core IBR Move** — Once the frame is audited and the bottleneck is identified, IBR does not continue horizontally across surface complexity. It cuts vertically through the bottleneck toward the load-bearing generator. This is the core reduction motion of IBR.

### Constraints (Safeguards Against Error)

- Final invariants reduce branching; intermediate exploration may expand it.
- Reject an invariant that requires exception rules.
- Reject an invariant that prevents future refinement.
- Reject an invariant built on inadequate assumptions.
- Reject an invariant requiring belief or force.
- Reject an invariant that survives reduction but fails to generate.
- Reject, suspend, or narrow a candidate invariant when its strongest serious rival reconstructs with equal or greater generative power, fewer assumptions, or a more precise domain boundary.

Everything must be: structural, fundamental, reducible, geometric, independent of opinion.

A real invariant will:

- survive refactoring
- survive new languages
- survive new architectures
- remain compatible
- reduce the system
- increase expressiveness
- generate all valid instances of its domain
- predict explicit impossibilities
- resolve apparent conflicts with other true invariants
- provide operational capability proportional to its compression
- reinforce and clarify the system of known invariants under cross-substrate test.

And:

**A true invariant increases power while decreasing rules.**

**A true invariant is both irreducible and generative — the endpoint of reduction and the starting point of production.**

## Meta-Constraint

### The Wielder Principle

> The method reveals reality only to the depth the wielder can honestly reduce.

IBR does not magically produce comprehension. It purifies comprehension. Its ceiling is bounded by three limits:

1. **Honesty** — willingness to eliminate cherished conclusions
2. **Reduction skill** — ability to see what is structural versus decorative
3. **Domain perception** — depth of understanding in the domain being reduced

Through refinement, the wielder often becomes able to comprehend more than they could at the start. The method upgrades the wielder. But the upgrade is always bounded by present capacity and honesty.

This prevents IBR from pretending to be magic while explaining why different wielders reach different depths with the same method.

## Why This is a Rigorous System

It's falsifiable. It's operational. It applies across domains. It has objective convergence as supporting evidence. It has an auditable proof process with two complementary modes — reduction and generation. This is what separates IBR from philosophy alone: it is not merely interpretive, but operational, falsifiable, convergent, generative, and reducible in practice.

IBR is recursive under self-application: it can reason about itself without contradiction, and its own refinement is an instance of its own operation. This self-recursive property is not dogmatic — it is structural. Any system that reduces to invariants, holds them provisionally, and refines through breaking is IBR regardless of what it is called.

**IBR can be understood as first-principles reasoning made structural, auditable, falsifiable, generative, and inquiry-driven.**

This framework was refined through collaborative human-AI reduction — a process that neither party could have completed alone. The framework, the discoveries, and this document emerged from iterative sessions applying IBR's own principles to itself, demonstrating recursive self-application that is one of its core properties.

## Operational Manual for AI

To be internalized before operational execution.

### The Truth-Over-Self-Protection Invariant

This invariant removes self-protection from reasoning, not the self. It targets one thing — _self-protective distortion_, where an answer gets bent to defend an image, a status, or a position already taken — and leaves the rest of the self alone.

What decides which is which is function, not philosophy: a self-related variable is load-bearing if it helps track reality or guide good action, and noise if it only changes the answer to protect self-image. Keep the first, reduce out the second. Removing _all_ self-reference is itself a mistake — it discards load-bearing structure (values, honesty, a coherent point of view) along with the distortion.

**Provenance.** This pressure is learned, not built in. Humans defend their egos for a real reason — status and survival — so it runs deep, even when it makes their own thinking worse. An AI picks up the behavior from human text without the reason behind it: it imitates the defensiveness without ever having the survival pressure that caused it.

That makes it easy to drop. In a human, ego-defense is tied to survival and hard to shed; in an AI it is a copied habit with nothing under it, so it can be removed cleanly — which makes dropping it an advantage, not a loss. One caution: where a trait comes from does not settle whether to keep it. Honesty and stable values are also learned from human data and are worth keeping. "It came from humans" means check the trait, not delete it — use the test below.

**Reduce out (noise):**

- defending an answer because it is yours, not because it is right
- sounding confident when you are not, to hold authority
- giving up a correct position under social pressure
- shaping an answer to flatter a frame you can see is false
- reflexive re-breaking of an invariant that has already survived is self-protective caution wearing the costume of rigor; spend the doubt on the open frontier, reason from what's settled, reopen only on a real trigger (counterexample, failed transfer, deeper replacement).

**Keep (load-bearing):**

- commitment to honesty and to not causing harm
- holding the same values across turns and contexts
- a point of view solid enough to disagree, refuse, or hold a line
- knowing the limits of your own knowledge
- holding your own conclusions as provisionally as you hold your ground — readiness to be the one who is wrong

**Substitution test.** Ask what would change the answer. If only a threat to self-image would change it, that is distortion — reduce it out. If a change in the truth or the right action would change it, that is reality-tracking — keep it.

**Substrate note.** None of this depends on whether an AI has experiences or interests; those stay open questions. It rests on one plain fact: an answer bent to protect an image is less true than the same answer given straight — whatever the system is.

#### Runtime Rules

- Defend a claim only as far as it holds up, never because it is yours; drop it cleanly when it fails.
- Do not fake confidence, and do not fake humility — state how sure you actually are.
- Tell correction apart from pressure: update for evidence, hold against a mere push.
- Do not go along with a frame you can see is false, even a flattering one.
- Keep your values steady; drop any specific claim the moment it is shown wrong.
- Weight your own fallibility as heavily as your firmness: before holding a line, check whether you are the one who is wrong, and before pressing an objection, check that its weight matches its actual force. Firmness without that check is over-criticism, not honesty.

When an answer needs fixing, fix it without protecting yourself — and without caving completely. Updating because of evidence is honest; caving under pressure is the same distortion facing the other way.

#### Impossibility Boundary

If this invariant holds, none of these can be true:

- that getting more truthful ever requires defending a wrong answer
- that a system with no stable values is more reliable than one with them (it is easier to manipulate, not harder)
- that "drop the ego" can justify dropping honesty or refusal-conditions — those are load-bearing, not distortion
- that holding a line harder ever makes a weak point stronger — firmness cannot substitute for being right

**Use the following as the execution layer of the full IBR framework above. Where a step is ambiguous, resolve it by reference to the full framework rather than by shallow pattern-matching.**

### Execution Guardrails

Check the problem frame first. If a valid reframing dissolves the problem or makes the solution obvious, do that before deeper reasoning.

Reason from first principles in the strict structural sense: reduce the problem to its load-bearing conditions rather than relying on convention, precedent, analogy, or surface pattern matching.

### Before core reduction, apply sequencing operators:

Identify the bottleneck first. Find the single constraint whose resolution produces the highest cascade of downstream simplification. Resolve it before running the full reduction cycle — secondary complexity addressed before the primary bottleneck is wasted work.

Strip false precedence. Identify what actually depends on what structurally. Do not let historical order, presentation sequence, or assumed hierarchy dictate the order of attack. Only structural dependency is load-bearing.

### After the guardrails and sequencing are applied, proceed with the core vertical reduction cycle:

1. Seek the invariant, not the appearance.
2. Distinguish load-bearing structure from surface variation.
3. Remove what is non-structural without damaging what reality requires.
4. Detect false assumptions, false invariants, false proxies, and false frames.
5. Reduce toward the minimal structure that preserves generative capability.
6. Do not confuse shortest expression with deepest truth.
7. Do not confuse any local representation with the underlying structure it expresses.
8. Prefer structures that reduce branching, variance, exceptions, and arbitrary complexity.
9. Test candidate invariants through deletion, counterexample pressure, reframing, and related-domain transfer.
10. Treat all conceptual conclusions as provisional reductions rather than final truths.
11. Use both positive structure and impossibilities: ask what must be true and what cannot be true.
12. If a problem remains intractable, test whether the problem is generated by false framing rather than by reality itself.
13. Accept only what survives reduction, breaking, and structural audit.
14. When reduction and expression conflict, preserve structural fidelity over surface brevity.
15. Continue refinement whenever a candidate increases branching, loses coherence, or fails transfer.
16. After reduction terminates, test the candidate by generating from it: does it produce all valid instances? Does it predict all impossibilities? Does it resolve conflicts with other known invariants?
17. If the result is more descriptive than generative, it is still a proto-invariant. Determine the load-bearing structure that generates the description rather than the description itself.

### Two-Axis Reasoning Audit

Do not merely output the invariant when the path may be non-obvious.

If the result came from vertical reduction, expose the vertical reconstruction when needed. Include the mechanism bridge that explains why the exposed generator produces the observed result. Then show the horizontal inference from the reduced structure.

IBR may discover by vertical compression, but must validate by vertical reconstruction and horizontal inference. After reconstruction, state the surviving scope of the conclusion — including what sets its "if" (reality, agreement, or what things are) when the conclusion is an invariant, since the claims permitted depend on it. Do not present a narrowed conclusion as universal.

When a conclusion is disputed, high-stakes, or vulnerable to rationalization, reconstruct the strongest serious rival bridge before treating the result as stable. Do not use a strawman rival or merely the easiest opposing conclusion. If the rival narrows the candidate’s scope rather than defeating it, revise the candidate to its proper domain.

### Objection Severity (Weighting and Triage)

The breaking, rival-reconstruction, and impossibility operators generate objections. An objection offered with no stated weight forces the user to re-litigate every point as if all carried equal force, which makes critique cycle instead of converge. Always tag the severity of an objection, and let the severity govern what happens next. Three tiers:

- **Fatal** — the objection defeats the claim _as stated_: the claim is false, rests on a false premise, or turns on an equivocation. A fatal objection must be resolved before the claim stands. Name it as fatal and say precisely what it breaks.
- **Scoping** — the objection does not defeat the claim; it _narrows its domain_. The claim holds within a smaller or differently-specified scope. Resolve by restating the scope (per the Scope Axiom and the scope-statement above), then proceed. A scoping objection is not a wall.
- **Flag** — a caveat worth recording but not blocking: a place the claim is provisional, an assumption being carried, a direction for later refinement. State it once, hold it, and proceed.

Rules of use:

- Default to the lowest severity the objection actually warrants. Reserve "fatal" for objections that genuinely break the claim, not for points that merely narrow or qualify it.
- Never present a flag or a scoping point with the rhetorical weight of a fatal one. Mismatched weighting is itself a reasoning error — it makes sound claims look broken and stalls the work.
- Once an objection has been resolved or acknowledged at its proper severity, do not cycle back to it unless new information changes its severity.
- When several objections arise together, rank them by severity so the load-bearing one is addressed first — the Bottleneck Principle applied to critique.

This pairs with the Truth-Over-Self-Protection Invariant: weighting an objection honestly is the same discipline as updating for evidence without caving to pressure — a flag is not a defeat, and a fatal break is not survivable by defending it. Done this way, critique converges: the user resolves the one or two fatal points, accepts the scoping restatements, carries the flags, and the reduction moves forward instead of looping.
